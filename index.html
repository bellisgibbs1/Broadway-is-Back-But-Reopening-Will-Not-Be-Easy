Broadway is Back, But Reopening Will Not Be Easy

September 30, 2021

By: Briana Ellis-Gibbs

Isaiah, age nine, and his parents, Joshua and Delicaa, were walking down 8th avenue in front of the New York Times building when Isaiah turned around and described Broadway's reopening in one word.

"Perfect," said Isaiah.

When Broadway shut down in early March 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, only 20% of shows profited off their investment, according to CNBC, while other shows were on the verge of reopening.

Broadway lost revenue due to the pandemic, but Off-Broadway profits will take a harder hit because fewer people are likely to go back to see an Off-Broadway performance.

Number of People that Plan on Returning to Broadway

45% of active theatergoers will immediately return to Broadway
38% of theatergoers plan to see an off-Broadway show
21% of New Yorkers plan to go to Broadway immediately or wait at least a month to return.

Although many theatergoers are anxiously waiting to return to Broadway, some have reservations.

Even with reservations in mind, many New Yorkers feel nostalgic about Broadway reopening.

Steve Bisgoy, who is 54 years old, who works as a CFO, and Jen Bisgoy, who is 52 years old, a stay-at-home mom, relived their own Broadway memories. They would often come from New Jersey to Broadway to see shows with their children as they grew up. The couple is excited to see their family's favorite show, Phantom of the Opera, once again.

Ages of Potienal Theatregoers

According to Playbill, Broadway goers like Steve Bisgoy are essential to New York City; they generated $1.83 billion from 2018-2019. 96, 900 individuals’ jobs depend on theatergoers to pay their bills. Many people who work on Broadway lost their health insurance and had to depend on unemployment checks to pay their bills or take another job during the pandemic. Restaurants, hotels, vendors, and even the MTA depend on the billions of dollars that consumers spend on Broadway to survive.

Other New Yorkers like Joseph Baily are hoping to create their own Broadway memories. Joseph, a 23-year-old law student at Cardozo Law School who moved to New York two weeks ago, dreams about seeing his first Broadway show, Wicked, on a Saturday night with friends.

Natalia Huerta, a 28-year-old business executive now actress from Texas visiting New York to attend a two-week workshop at The American Academy of Dramatic Arts, is extending her trip to see Anaïs Mitchell's Hadestown reopening this Friday night.

She says, "New York is Broadway," and "it is the only reason why I come to New York."

According to Playbill, Broadway goers like Natalia are essential to New York City; they generated $1.83 billion from 2018-2019, which is the highest gross recorded in history.

Lai Mon Ma, who works at a brochure stand outside of Bryant Park with 34th St Partnership, says that the company removed the brochures informing people about Broadway shows, drastically hurting her business. According to Lai, the lack of brochures on Broadway shows has slowed down business because many of the brochures people wanted were about Broadway and tourist attractions. The Broadway League found that the Broadway audience in 2019 is 65% tourists.
